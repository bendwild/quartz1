# My thoughts
"An Imperfect Offering" is the first book I have been able to finish in [[2025]] and I feel like I'm going to need some time to fully digest the facts and messages of this book. While not any special in its writing, James Orbinski shares with us a painfully raw account of his life story as a humanitarian doctor. He recounts working during the Rwandan genocide, where almost 1 million Tutsi's (and moderate Hutsi's) were killed and 2 million displaced in a span of 100 days, while the world governments looked on, refusing to take the word genocide in their mouth. He writes about Afghanistan where imperial ambitions of a post-9/11 America intentionally induced human suffering in their ideological so-called fight against "evil". North Korea, Kosovo, Sudan, Somalia... Different countries, different pasts, different suffering but one thing remains; colonial/imperial roots can be found in many of these conflicts and humanitarian aid will only be effectively distributed to these countries if it coincides with the political objectives of neo-colonial/post-imperial military interventions and capitalist interests. But ok, tell me something I didn't know... 

What I want to take with me is how, confronted by this immense unjust suffering, James tells the story of his [[Revolt against the absurd]], and how the only response that made sense is to act from a feeling of shared vulnerability and bear the responsibility of being witness and telling the world. To affirm your own nature in a place that is absent of any meaning, not for any clicks or likes, but from a universal humanity, from [[Ubuntu]], from humility; that is the only *imperfect offering* you can bring. 

"I fight not for a utopian ideal, but each day I make a choice, against [[nihilism]] and towards justice."

# Highlights

"An Imperfect Offering is about finding a way to confront unjust human suffering in the world as it is. Today one of the greatest challenges facing humanitarianism is the blurring of boundaries between humanitarian assistance and the political objectives of military intervention. In the post–Cold War world, we have seen selective military humanitarian interventions in Somalia, East Timor, Kosovo, Haiti and Sierra Leone. But we have not seen the same actions taken in Angola, Chechnya, Nepal or North Korea. In these and many other countries, egregious violations of human rights and the laws of war have occurred or are occurring, causing profound and widespread human suffering."
