# What is it?
- originally developed for **policy research**
- part of family of analysis methods called **thematic analysis/qualitative content analysis**

# Process
## 1. Familiarization
- reviewing fieldnotes
- listening to audio recordings
- open coding

> [!ATTENTION] Especially important when analyst did not collect a portion of/all of the data!

## 2. Identifying a thematic framework
- finalizing codebook
- codes can be grouped together into categories (similar to axial coding)
- important for all study analysts to be involved with this step of the research process
## 3. Indexing
- applying finalized codes to the transcripts
- qualitative analysis **may be iterative or not**
	- while indexing you may need to refine framework
	- however happens after data sampling, you don’t take your reflections back to the data analysis (purely in indexing) ≠ [[Grounded theory]] [[Inductive coding]] 
## 4. Charting
- creating framework table or other form of [[data visualization]]
	1. Excerpts
	2. Summaries
	3. Tables
- CAVE make a table that is informative but not unwieldly

## 5. Mapping and interpretation
- identify patterns in the data and cross-cutting themes
- identify commonalities and differences across cases
- draw descriptive or explanatory conclusions clustered around themes

> [!ATTENTION]  Emphasis is on **identifying thematic conclusions**
> - FA makes it easy to distinguish between major and minor themes

> [!Tip] Framework analysis pros & cons
> **Pro**
> - clear steps to follow
> - helpful for managing a large data set with clear domains
>   
> **Cons**
> - some steps can feel tedious
> - new researchers can be unsure what to present in the final paper (tendency to present everything)

| **Analytic steps** | 1. Familiarization<br>2. Open coding<br>3. Focused coding (axial)<br>4. Analytical memoing<br>5. Thematic framework<br>4. Indexing<br>5. Charting<br>6. Mapping and interpretation<br>7. Report writing                                                                       |
| **Analytic goals** | Understanding emerges from the close study of texts<br>- **analysis + data collection at the same time** (theoretical sampling)<br>- requires interaction between data collection and analysis<br>- analytic process leads to **theory development** | Understanding emerges from the close study of texts<br>- analysis after data collection<br>- focus on identifying **thematic conclusions**, appropriate for describing major themes in qualitative studies         |
| **Data analysis**  | Systematic, iterative flexible method<br>- coding, memoing and theorizing *as you go*<br>- integrating, refining and writing up theories                                                                                                             | - create a framework analysis table (cross-case and within-case comparisons)<br>- identify commonalities and differences in qualitative data<br>- draw descriptive/explanatory conclusions clustered around themes |
| **Challenges**     | - if theory already developed<br>- if short research time horizon<br>- if research data already collected<br>- data saturation makes it so you can’t set # of participants beforehand                                                                | - may constrain flexibility and creativity  <br>- relies on pre-existing frameworks, which can [[bias]] interpretation  <br>- less focus on new theory development                                                     |
| **When to use?**   | - when no existing theory explains the phenomenon  <br>- when aiming to develop a new conceptual framework or model  <br>- when data collection and analysis can occur iteratively                                                                   | - theory or framework is already developed at the outset of the study<br>- data analysis does not begin until after data collection is complete                                                                    |
