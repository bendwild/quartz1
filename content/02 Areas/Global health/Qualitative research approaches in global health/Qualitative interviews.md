# Types of interviews
- structured
	- all questions are asked as written in the guide
	- all participants are asked the same questions
	- no flexibility from the interviewer
- semi-structured
	- interview guide with pre-written question
	- flexibility to drop, add, re-order or re-word questions as appropiate
	- most common in global health
- unstructured
	- no pre-specified questions
	- conversation is used for **hypothesis generation** (getting preliminary understanding of a situation)

# Preparation
- conduct background research
- clarify your aims
- identify interview informants
- develop [[#Interview questions]]
	- encourage narrative (try to ask questions that get the participant to tell you a story)
		- helps avoid the trap of getting only short answers
		- discourages the participant from doing the analysis for you
- identify space(s) for interviewing
## Interview questions
### General tips
- use open-ended questions (you need descriptive answers, **maximize narrative**)
- avoid leading questions (biases)
- use language that the participant can understand
- keep the questions concise (short & specific)
- avoid asking two-in-one questions (singular)
- keep questions neutral (avoid framing questions with positive/negative associations)
- use probes (to elaborate on themes clarify concepts)

> [!attention] 
> - Use words like: "describe," "characterize," "understand," "explore"
> - **Avoid words** like "measure," "quantify," "prevalence," "frequency"

### Question techniques
- split up questions
- role playing and simulation questions
- transitioning and summarizing
- prefacing questions (kaderen)
- offering support and recognition

> [!DANGER] Avoid
> - double-barelled questions
> - compound questions
> - why questions
> - leading questions

## Sequencing considerations
- question hierarchy
	- start with experiences (what happened)
	- build to opinions and values (what they think/feel)
	- handle knowledge questions sensitively (don't make it feel like a quiz)
- consider existing theory
	- keep in mind… qualitative research is iterative, you still may be informed by existing theory at the study outset
- start with easier questions to get the person talking
- queries should build from the content that has come earlier in the interview
	- you want the interview to feel like a conversation
- group questions on each domain/topic together
	- within domains/narratives, start with most concrete issues, then move to more abstract
- place sensitive questions later in the interview
	- depending on why you recruited the participant
- try to end on a positive note

# During the interview

## Introduction
- set up expectations for the interview
- discuss consent form, confidentiality, purpose

## Rapport-building
= the ability to (quickly) create interviewer/participant dynamics that are positive, relaxed and mutually respectful
- Establish comfort and respect
- Emphasize the participant’s perspective
- Adapt to different personalities and emotional states
- Ask questions
- Use culturally-specific styles and techniques for building rapport
- Probing
	- echo probe
	- agreement/acknowledgement
	- ask for opinion/more information/clarification
	- keep the participant on track
	- sustain a narrative and explore it further
	- non-verbal
- Watch for body language
	- maintain neutral affect
	- warm but not biased

> [!ATTENTION] Rapport building is critical to any interview!
> - participants will talk freely, openly and honestly if the feel comfortable and trust the interviewer

## Common pitfalls
- maintaining control of the interview
- know how to tone down heightened emotions
- running out of time
- avoid why questions
## Field notes
- distinguish interpretation from observation
- avoid vague and overgeneralized statements
- make detailed and concrete notes
