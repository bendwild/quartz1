# Coding

> [!ATTENTION] What is a code?
> = unit of meaning
> - a priori codes
> 	- developed in advance by existing theory or concepts (theory-driven) 
> 	- derived from a specific project’s research goals and questions (structural))
> - emergent codes
> 	- emerge from the data (data-driven, inductive)

![[Pasted image 20251019170444.png]]
## 1. Familiarization with the data
- close reading of text

## 2. First stage of coding
**= open coding**
- code data line by line
- remain open to possible themes, concepts and categories that emerge
- **develop potential codes and labels**
- fine-level vs big-bucket coding

> [!TIP] Tips
> - listen closely and respond to your data in an open-minded way
> - move quickly, keep codes simple and precise
> - use gerunds (-ing)
> - no abstract themes or theory, **stay close to the data**
> - capture enough context (lumper vs splitter)

## 3. Second stage of coding
**= focused coding**
- pinpoint and develop most salient themes and categories
- refine codes by reviewing, re-coding and testing against new data
- **axial coding**
	- consolidate codes and sort into groups (hierarchical or non-hierarchical)
- find the patterns in your data

> [!TIP] What to look for when you’re coding?
> - What is this saying? What does it represent?
> - What is this an example of?
> - What are people doing?
> - What kind of events are occurring
> - What is trying to be conveyed?
> - How does this relate to my research question?

## 4. Analytical stage
- prepare analytical memo (including excerpts from original data)
- = basis of results section

> [!attention] [[Grounded theory]] coding
> = analysis as an interaction
> - requires us to stop and ask analytic questions of the data we have gathered
> - continues the interaction that you shared with your participants while collecting data (second interaction with your analytical question)
> - iterative process beyond indexing, you take it with you in further data sampling ≠ [[Framework analysis]]

> [!info] Two-stage participant interaction
> - First interaction: during data collection (interviews)
> - Second interaction: during coding (analytical conversation with data)
> - Coding involves asking analytical questions of the data
> - Researcher becomes part of the analysis process in stage 2

# Codebook
= detailed description of each code and usage guidelines
- allows consistent use of codes 
- ideally, someone should be able to pick up your codebook and apply it to transcripts


> [!IMPORTANT] Essential components for a quality codebook
> - mutually exclusive codes (avoid thematic overlap)
> - ensure good intercoder reliability through clear definitions (clear inclusion and exclusion criteria)
> - “lumper” vs “splitter” approach (capture enough context to understand your coding decisions)
> - examples of usage
